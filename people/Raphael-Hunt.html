Raphael Hunt 

Creative programming has made possible the formal exploration of heretofore impossible modes of audio-visual production. OpenFrameworks, P5.js and Processing all readily offer various configurements of possible inputs (raw data, users, performers, etc), outputs (visuals sounds, etc) and processing methods (that exist in the program / the work). Thus interactivity  has emerged as a new and novel frontier for the computer artist to explore. Interactivity, as considered a new formal quality for art, in turn assumes, complicates and challenges boundless questions about the art object, its relationship to the viewer and the aesthetic experience. Using P5.js, Raphael Hunt programs visual patterns with the goal of activating the psychosomatic effects of color and shape. In his work, Human Trial, phenomena such as strobe and moire patterns are sustained to oblige the audience into being conscious of the sensation of seeing. The eye becomes the literal threshold between the viewer and the intensity of the flashing images in front of them. In Human Trial, interactivityâ€™s aesthetic capacity is exploded by calling into question the seamless reciprocation between user and machine that is sought after and encouraged by digital technology. By removing the actively responsive apparatus between user and machine, Hunt reconfigures interactivity as an antagonistic relationship between viewer and image.  Without any interface to mediate the data being transmitted between user and screen, the viewer effectively becomes receptacle for a deluge of visual information. In mediums such as film or literature this information was arranged to be manageable. Creative programming on the other hand grants the synthesis of visual information at a rate faster than can be manageably interpreted. The soothing, hypnotizing, or overwhelming states that Human Trial may induce are all consequential to an overload of visual information. In rendering complex strobe sequences and moire patterns for Human Trial, the software used to edit on as well as the browser running P5.js would lag and glitch, a possible parallel to the over load of information in the view.
